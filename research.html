Research

My research investigates how ancient communities transformed environments and how modern geospatial methods can help reconstruct those transformations. I work across archaeology, geography, environmental science, remote sensing, and digital heritage, with the Maya lowlands as my primary field context.

Ancient Engineered Landscapes

This work examines reservoirs, canals, dams, terraces, plazas, civic architecture, causeways, settlement systems, and modified terrain as evidence for ancient landscape design. Current and future projects include Laguna Seca, Booth’s River canal coring and dating, GPR and volumetric fill modeling, and lidar-guided settlement analysis.

Environmental Legacies

This research reconstructs long-term environmental change through lake cores, wetland soils, sediment records, vegetation change, fire severity, and landscape degradation. Projects include Laguna Verde, Wamil, Río Azul, and post-fire vegetation regrowth research in southern Belize.

Digital Geospatial Heritage

This work develops workflows for accessible and advanced digital documentation, including UAV lidar, airborne lidar, terrestrial laser scanning, mobile lidar, photogrammetry, 3D printing, virtual reality, and digital twins for research, conservation, teaching, and public engagement.

CAVE Lab

The CAVE Lab—Computational Archaeology and Visualized Environments—studies how people transform environments across time. Through lidar, drone mapping, 3D scanning, geophysics, excavation, coring, soil and sediment analysis, remote sensing, VR, and 3D printing, the lab documents ancient engineered landscapes and their modern environmental legacies.
