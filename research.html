Research

Ancient engineered landscapes, environmental legacies, and digital geospatial heritage.

My research asks how ancient communities transformed environments and how modern geospatial methods can help us reconstruct, interpret, and share those landscapes.

Research Identity

I am a geospatial geoarchaeologist who uses lidar, drones, 3D modeling, excavation, coring, soils, paleoenvironmental data, and digital visualization to study long-term human–environment interaction in the Maya lowlands. My work treats landscapes not as passive settings for human activity, but as dynamic records of engineering, adaptation, memory, risk, and environmental change.

Ancient Engineered Landscapes

This research examines reservoirs, canals, dams, terraces, plazas, civic architecture, and settlement systems as evidence for ancient landscape design. Projects include Laguna Seca, Booth’s River canal coring and dating, GPR and volumetric fill modeling, and lidar-guided settlement analysis.

Environmental Legacies

This work reconstructs long-term environmental change through lake cores, wetland soils, sediment records, vegetation change, fire severity, and landscape degradation. Projects include Laguna Verde, Wamil, Río Azul, and post-fire vegetation regrowth research in southern Belize.

Digital Geospatial Heritage

This research develops workflows for accessible and advanced digital documentation, including UAV lidar, airborne lidar, TLS, mobile lidar, photogrammetry, 3D printing, virtual reality, and digital twins for research, conservation, teaching, and public engagement.

Current and Future Directions

Laguna Seca and Laguna Verde, Belize

Integrating lidar, excavation, coring, bathymetry, radiocarbon dating, and paleoenvironmental reconstruction to understand civic landscape construction, water management, and long-term environmental change in northwestern Belize.

UAV and Airborne Lidar in Maya Archaeology

Comparing drone and traditional airborne lidar for archaeological terrain modeling across vegetation and land-management contexts, with attention to workflow, visibility, resolution, classification, and field interpretation.

“Negative Space” and Lidar Expectations

Exploring how lidar changes archaeological expectations by revealing not only dense settlement, but also areas of apparent absence, lower-density occupation, or survey bias.

Digital Twins and Landscape Legacies

Developing integrated digital records that combine lidar, TLS, drone imagery, hyperspectral or multispectral data, botanical surveys, and community knowledge to study ancient landscapes and their modern environmental legacies.
