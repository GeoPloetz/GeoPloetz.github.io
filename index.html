Chris J. Ploetz is a geospatial geoarchaeologist whose research examines ancient engineered landscapes, environmental legacies, and human–environment interaction in the Maya lowlands. His work integrates lidar, drone mapping, GIS, 3D modeling, excavation, coring, soils, paleoenvironmental reconstruction, and digital heritage to reconstruct how communities transformed tropical landscapes over time.

Chris’s current research focuses on ancient Maya landscapes in Belize and Guatemala, including civic construction, water management, wetlands, settlement patterns, lidar-based archaeological prospection, and the environmental traces of long-term land use. He is especially interested in how computational and geospatial methods can make archaeological and environmental records more accessible for research, teaching, conservation, and public engagement.

His future research program is organized through the CAVE Lab—Computational Archaeology and Visualized Environments—which connects field-based archaeology, geospatial science, environmental analysis, and immersive digital visualization.

Current Research Themes

  • Ancient engineered landscapes, including reservoirs, canals, terraces, civic architecture, and settlement systems.
  • Environmental legacies preserved in wetlands, lake cores, soils, vegetation, fire histories, and land-cover change.
  • Digital geospatial heritage, including lidar, drone mapping, mobile scanning, 3D modeling, VR, StoryMaps, and 3D printing.
