CAVE Lab

Computational Archaeology and Visualized Environments.

The CAVE Lab is a future-facing research and teaching identity focused on ancient landscapes, geospatial science, environmental change, and immersive visualization.

Lab Mission

The CAVE Lab studies how people transform environments across time. Through lidar, drone mapping, 3D scanning, geophysics, excavation, coring, soil and sediment analysis, remote sensing, VR, and 3D printing, the lab documents ancient engineered landscapes and their modern environmental legacies.

Methods

  • Airborne and UAV lidar
  • Terrestrial laser scanning and mobile lidar
  • GIS, spatial analysis, terrain modeling, and cartography
  • Drone mapping, multispectral imagery, and future hyperspectral workflows
  • Excavation, coring, soils, sediment analysis, and geoarchaeology
  • GPR, bathymetric mapping, 3D printing, VR, and digital twins

Student Training

The lab is designed around hands-on learning. Students should leave with practical experience, portfolio-ready projects, and confidence using field, lab, and digital tools. Training opportunities may include GIS, drone mapping, lidar processing, 3D documentation, excavation, coring, environmental sampling, digital storytelling, and public-facing heritage work.

Research Themes

Ancient Engineered Landscapes

How did ancient communities build, maintain, and modify terrain, water systems, civic spaces, and settlement landscapes?

Environmental Legacies

How do soils, wetlands, forests, lakes, and sediment records preserve the long-term consequences of human activity?

Digital Heritage

How can 3D models, StoryMaps, VR, and digital twins support interpretation, conservation, teaching, and public engagement?

Future Field School Vision

One future goal is to develop a formal field-school experience in Belize connected to archaeology, geography, environmental science, drone mapping, agricultural landscapes, and cultural immersion. I have discussed the potential for future student field training with the Maya Mountain Research Farm, where existing infrastructure could support a hands-on program that connects geospatial methods, environmental science, and Maya cultural landscapes.
