Teaching

Hands-on learning for real-world problems.

My teaching is built around the idea that students learn best when they can connect concepts to practical tools, field experiences, and problems that matter beyond the classroom.

Teaching Philosophy

I am passionate about helping students gain hands-on experience and practical skills that they can take into whatever they do next. Whether students continue in archaeology, geography, environmental science, GIS, cultural resource management, public service, or another path entirely, I want them to leave my classes with confidence, curiosity, and a stronger sense of what they can contribute.

In my teaching, I scaffold technical methods so that students who may not initially see themselves as “field people,” “GIS people,” or “researchers” can build competence step by step. I emphasize clear expectations, applied assignments, collaborative learning, and opportunities to work with authentic spatial, environmental, and archaeological data.

Inclusive Field and Technical Training

I have supported students who had never been in the field before and students who were not classically trained as archaeologists but had meaningful skills, perspectives, and curiosity to bring to field programs. I see fieldwork and technical training as opportunities to widen access, not to gatekeep expertise.

In future courses and field programs, I aim to create pathways for students to gain experience with mapping, excavation, drones, lidar, 3D modeling, environmental sampling, digital storytelling, and community-engaged research.
